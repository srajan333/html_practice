Following Palmer's first win (and expected lunch with then-President Dwight D. Eisenhower[1]) with Avery at the 1958 Masters Tournament, Palmer's wife Winnie meant to write a check to Avery for the then-standard $1,400 caddie fee, but in her haste instead wrote the amount as $14,000; Avery unsuccessfully tried to cash the check, calling her from Augusta National.[2][6] At the 1960 Masters Tournament, with Palmer down at the 15th hole, Avery asked, "Mr. Palmer, are we chokin'?"[2] Palmer then shot two birdies on the 17th and 18th to win the tournament by a single stroke.[2] Palmer would later remark: [Avery's] understanding of what made me tick was perhaps instinctive and definitely profound. I stared back at him and realized he was right—I was foolishly beating on myself instead of taking care of the business of playing the golf course. His scowl was eerily reminiscent of the disapproving glare Pap used to give me as a kid whenever I threw a club or failed to keep my mind on the job.[1]

Primiere

Avery and Palmer's last tournament together was in 1968, and Palmer lost touch with Avery over time.[2] In 1969, Avery told the AP, "I was with Arnie through four championships and then he let me down."[8] By 1974, the AP found Avery "sad" and "lonely" in Augusta,[4] having last worked as a caddie in 1971 and only able to find work at the Masters carrying equipment for a photographer. Avery told them "no man knows this course like me. I know every tree, every blade of grass, every break on the greens."[4] Avery also said Palmer was "having his problems. He'll never make a comeback until he gets me back, you just watch and see."[4] Avery also often spent winters in New York with relatives, and was briefly incarcerated in Richmond County Jail.[1][7]

Solo Series

Nathaniel "Iron Man" Avery (1939 – May 3, 1985[1][2]) was an African-American golf caddie at Augusta National Golf Club, famous for helping Arnold Palmer win four Masters titles in 1958, 1960, 1962, and 1964.[2][3] His high public profile and visibility during Palmer's rise to fame made Avery one of the first caddies given attention by the media.[1]

Fictional Character Biography

Avery was born in Augusta, Georgia, in 1939, the last of his parents' eight children.[4][2] His older brother "Big Henry" Avery was caddie master at the Augusta Country Club, leading Avery himself to spend time at the caddie yard in his early teens.[2] During this time in the early 1950s, Avery would often boast he could easily carry the heaviest bags, first earning him the nickname "Iron Man".[a][2] Avery began caddying for then-rookie Arnold Palmer in 1954 at the age of 16 – "Even though Ken Venturi was the heat," he would later tell the Augusta Chronicle.[1][4][6]
